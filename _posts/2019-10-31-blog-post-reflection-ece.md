I hated taking chemistry
======
I actually came in as a Mechanical Engineering major, and in retrospect I have no idea why. I think I had this thought that Mechanical Engineering was somehow the broadest field of engineering. Either that, or it had something to do with my love of Legos as a child. First semester went by, and I was looking for ways to never have to take chemistry again (MECH requires 2 semesters!). As I was browsing through the engineering handbook they gave us during orientation week, I came to the amazing discovery that the electrical engineering department only required first semester chemistry! Not only could I stop taking my most dreaded subject, my semester of torture would not go to waste. 

So, without thinking too much of it, I called my mother and told her that I decided to switch majors. As an aside this really _wasn't_ a big deal, but I had been slightly nervous during that phone call. Pre-college me had been consistently exposed to my mother comforting family friends about their college-level son/daughter not being able to to decide what they wanted to study. So for some reason I had this mentality that major switch = bad despite literally every academic advisor telling you otherwise during freshman year. 

My ego gets knocked down a few notches
======
So, as a newly self-proclaimed electrical engineering major I started taking the intro level programming course that spring of my freshman year. I was having a lot of fun at first. As a kid (and maybe sometimes, even now) my favorite game was [ROBLOX](https://www.roblox.com){:target="_blank"}. tl;dr it's a game where you can build games for other people to play, and while it's technically marketed towards kids, the game engine is fairly involved and includes scripting in [Lua](https://www.lua.org){:target="_blank"} for complex gameplay. Kid me had always been too impatient to learn, and I remember I would dream of creating all these cool games when I finally learned how to code. 

Midterm season comes in, and I felt fully prepared for the coding exam (we used a goofy Python environment called [CodeSkulptor](https://py3.codeskulptor.org){:target="_blank"}) as I casually scrolled through the previous homeworks to review what I had learned. Since there was an autograder, I knew my score right when I finished, and whether it was nerves or my lack of preperation, I absolutely __bombed__ the test. I remember feeling absolutely devestated. How could I, a top 10% student at my high school have done so poorly (25 points below average, yikes!)? 

![My exam score X)](/images/comp130.png)

__First lesson at university:__ there is always someone way smarter than you. I remember _crying_ on the phone with my mom right after. In that moment, I genuinely wanted to just go home. Screw the prestige of going to a top university, what did it matter if I couldn't even pass a freshman-level class? I don't remember what went on the week or two after that, but after talking with a friend from my high-school who went through the exact same situation, I resolved to do much betterm, even if it meant I had to work twice as hard as others to catch up

Long story short I consider this experience pivotal for me because it: 
1. Humbled my existence at the university
2. Pushed me to develop smarter studying habits

This probably one of those "discovering yourself" in college moments.

Other kinds of doubts
======
Sophomore year comes in, and I'm fairly optimistic about school despite having to commute by bike (the [residential colleges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residential_colleges_of_Rice_University) at Rice generally "kick" students off campus at least once in 4 years). Around then I started noticing a trend: the computer science department was blowing up with more and more students (Algorithms enrollment had nearly _doubled_ in size). Graduating students were getting cool jobs at big tech companies. Wait, I want that too! Driven by my vision of future clout, I called my parents to tell them I wanted to switch majors _again_. Remember what I was saying earlier about my mother/switching majors? I got an earful. In the end, somehow, my parents convinced me not to change. I reluctantly agreed and sophomore year started.
