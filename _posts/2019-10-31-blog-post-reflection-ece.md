This blog post is just some rambling, and is not very well written.

I hated taking chemistry
======
I actually came in as a Mechanical Engineering major, and in retrospect I have no idea why. First semester went by, and I was looking for ways to never have to take chemistry again (MECH requires 2 semesters!). As I was browsing through the engineering handbook they gave us during orientation week, I came to the amazing discovery that the electrical engineering department only required first semester chemistry! Not only could I stop taking my most dreaded subject, my semester of torture would not go to waste.

So, without thinking too much of it, I called my mother and told her that I decided to switch majors. As an aside this really _wasn't_ a big deal, but I had been slightly nervous during that phone call. Pre-college me had been consistently exposed to my mother comforting family friends about their college-level son/daughter not being able to to decide what they wanted to study. So for some reason I had this mentality that major switch = bad despite literally every academic advisor telling you otherwise during freshman year. 

I learn about putting in work
======
So, as a newly self-proclaimed electrical engineering major I started taking the intro level programming course that spring of my freshman year. I was having a lot of fun at first, I had always wanted to be able to code. Growing up, one of my favorite games was [ROBLOX](https://www.roblox.com){:target="_blank"}. tl;dr it's a game where you can build games for other people to play, and while it's technically marketed towards kids, the game engine is fairly involved and includes scripting in [Lua](https://www.lua.org){:target="_blank"} for complex gameplay. Kid me had always been too impatient to actually sit down and learn to program, and I remember I would dream of creating all these cool games when I finally learned how to code. 

Midterm season comes in, and I felt fully prepared for the coding exam (we used a goofy Python environment called [CodeSkulptor](https://py3.codeskulptor.org){:target="_blank"}) as I casually scrolled through the previous homeworks to review what I had learned. Since there was an autograder, I knew my score right when I finished exam 1, and whether it was nerves or my lack of preperation, I absolutely __bombed__ the test. I remember feeling absolutely devestated. How could I, a top 10% student at my high school have done so poorly (25 points below average, yikes!)? 

![My exam score X)](/images/comp130.png)

__First lesson at university:__ there is always someone way smarter than you. I remember _crying_ on the phone with my mom right after. In that moment, I genuinely wanted to just go home. Screw the prestige of going to a top university, what did it matter if I couldn't even pass a freshman-level class? I don't remember what went on the week or two after that, but after talking with a friend from my high-school who went through the exact same situation, I resolved to do much better, even if it meant I had to work twice as hard as others to catch up.

Long story short I consider this experience pivotal for me because it: 
1. Humbled my existence at the university
2. Pushed me to develop smarter studying habits

Other kinds of doubts
======
Sophomore year comes in, and I'm fairly optimistic about school despite having to commute by bike (the [residential colleges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residential_colleges_of_Rice_University) at Rice generally "kick" students off campus at least once in 4 years). Around then I started noticing a trend: the computer science department was blowing up with more and more students (Algorithms enrollment had nearly _doubled_ in size). Graduating students were getting cool jobs at big tech companies. Wait, I want that too! Driven by my vision of future clout, I called my parents to tell them I wanted to switch majors _again_. Remember what I was saying earlier about my mother/switching majors? I got an earful. In the end, somehow, my parents convinced me not to change. I reluctantly agreed and sophomore year started.

Wait, what do ELECs do?
======
Sophomore year is when electrical engineering students at Rice typically begin actual EE classes. So in the Fall of 2017 I started taking the vaguely named "Fundamentals of Electrical Engineering 1". I came in expecting circuits, and what I got was an introduction to signals and systems, a field I had no idea existed. Historically, Rice has been a leader in signal processing. For example, the [Parks-McClellan FIR filter design algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parks–McClellan_filter_design_algorithm) was developed at Rice, and a few of the professors in our department are titans in the world of DSP. That semester, I also took an introductory quantum physics/materials course and digital logic design. All of this is to say that I had a _very_ diverse introduction to the world of electrical engineering. In retrospect, I am so glad I stayed in the department instead of switching majors. I think that semester is what sparked my love of _learning for the sake of learning_. 

During my time at Rice I've taken a lot of "useless" classes. __Defined as:__ I will never use this in any future endeavour or career. And to tell you the truth it really bothers me when people tout about how they "never used anything they learned in college" and that university is just for the piece of paper (diploma). Now I recognize that there are a diverse range of personal motivators for saying something like this (financial is probably a big one). But I think it's kind of sad that higher education is often viewed like this. I see every class I take as:

1. An opportunity to learn something new and "broaden my horizons" (big cringe)
2. An exercise in problem solving ability, something that I think actually is practical for an engineer

For me, having that education for the sake of learning is enough. Who knows, maybe one day I'll need [recall why wires/cables carrying high-frequency AC consist of many smaller wires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skin_effect).
